# Comparison Template

Structured format for `/compare` output. Adapt to the subject and concepts being compared.

## Standard Structure

### 1. At a Glance

| | Concept A | Concept B |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | $...$ | $...$ |
| **Key property** | ... | ... |
| **Notation** | ... | ... |
| **Typical context** | ... | ... |

### 2. Key Differences

Use a numbered list. Each difference should be:
- Specific (not "they're different")
- Illustrated with math or an example when possible
- Stated in terms of WHAT differs and WHY it matters

### 3. Similarities

What they share. Often more subtle than differences:
- Common ancestor concept
- Cases where they coincide
- Shared notation or structure

### 4. The Critical Counterexample

The ONE example that makes the distinction crystal clear. This is the most important part of any comparison. Format:

> **The example**: [concrete mathematical/scientific object]
> - Concept A: [holds / doesn't hold] because [reason]
> - Concept B: [holds / doesn't hold] because [reason]
> - **Takeaway**: [what this tells us about the difference]

### 5. When to Use Which

Practical decision guide:
- "Use A when..." / "Use B when..."
- "The distinction matters when..." / "It doesn't matter when..."

### 6. Common Confusion Points

What students typically mix up:
- Notational similarities that mask conceptual differences
- Verbal descriptions that sound the same but mean different things
- Edge cases where intuition fails

## Subject-Specific Adaptations

**Math**: emphasize logical conditions, quantifier order, domain restrictions
**Physics**: emphasize units, physical interpretation, experimental signatures
**Chemistry**: emphasize conditions (temperature, pressure, phase), reaction mechanisms
**Statistics**: emphasize assumptions, when each test/method is appropriate, interpretation differences
